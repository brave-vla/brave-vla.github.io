About
Abstract
Recent advances in Vision-Language models (VLMs) have enabled the development of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models, with strong results on curated benchmarks such as LIBERO, SimplerEnv, and Meta-World. However, real-world reliability remains limited. In applications such as household mobile manipulation, public-space assistance, and warehouse logistics, models must generalize and be robust under occlusions, changing viewpoints, novel objects, and enable long-horizon task execution. Beyond control difficulties, recent studies reveal limitations such as sensitivity to appearance and distribution shifts, which reduces robustness, and shortcut learning from spurious visual cues and dataset bias, which leads to weak language grounding -- where models fail to connect linguistic instructions to the relevant visual cues.

This workshop focuses on VLA in embodied AI with an emphasis on real-world reliability. One central question is: what are the concrete bottlenecks -- in representation, data, evaluation, and system integration -- for robust VLA systems in realistic settings?

We propose a half-day workshop consisting of five keynote talks, contributed poster presentations, and a panel discussion. Speakers are from both academia and industry, bringing diverse perspectives on foundation models, vision-language representations, and robotic systems. This workshop brings together the computer vision, language, and robotics communities to align on shared problem definitions, highlight common failure modes, and discuss emerging approaches toward building deployable and robust embodied systems in real-world scenarios.
